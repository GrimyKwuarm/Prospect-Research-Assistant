# Source Quality Rubric

## Reliability Tiers

### Primary

Use for official or direct sources:

- official UC source,
- official organisation website,
- public registry,
- annual report,
- public filing,
- court or regulatory record,
- direct user-provided source of record,
- internal source authorised for use.

### Strong Secondary

Use for credible but non-primary sources:

- reputable news outlet,
- established sector publication,
- credible interview,
- recognised award or event page,
- well-maintained professional profile.

### Secondary

Use for ordinary third-party summaries:

- directory profile,
- event listing,
- public biography hosted by a third party,
- conference speaker page,
- non-official organisational summary.

### Weak

Use for sources that should not carry important claims alone:

- unattributed pages,
- scraped directories,
- outdated aggregators,
- promotional fragments,
- unsourced summaries,
- pages with unclear authorship.

### Unknown

Use when reliability cannot be assessed.

## Recency

- `current`: direct present-status source or updated within 12 months.
- `recent`: 1-3 years old and still plausibly relevant.
- `dated`: 3-5 years old; use cautiously.
- `stale`: more than 5 years old or clearly superseded.
- `unknown`: no date is available.

Current roles, directorships, employment, leadership, financial data, and sensitivities should not rely on stale or unknown recency without cautious wording or review.

## Subject Match

- `direct`: source clearly names and identifies the intended subject.
- `probable`: source likely refers to the subject but lacks full disambiguation.
- `ambiguous`: multiple possible subjects or unclear identity.
- `no_match`: source does not appear to relate to the subject.

Ambiguous subject match is a stop sign for final-profile claims.

## Evidence Strength

- `strong`: primary source or multiple reinforcing credible sources.
- `adequate`: credible source support, but not primary or multi-source.
- `limited`: one plausible but incomplete, dated, or indirect source.
- `weak`: unreliable, vague, unattributed, stale, or ambiguous source.
- `none`: no source support.

## Confidence Assignment

Use `high` only when:

- subject match is direct,
- evidence is strong or adequate,
- source is current, recent, or authoritative,
- no unresolved contradiction exists,
- privacy and sensitivity checks pass.

Use `medium` when:

- source support is plausible but not definitive,
- one credible source supports the claim,
- recency is dated or mixed,
- wording must be cautious.

Use `low` when:

- source support is weak,
- identity match is ambiguous,
- source is stale or unknown for current information,
- the claim is inferential,
- conflicting evidence exists.

Use `excluded` when:

- no evidence supports the claim,
- stronger evidence contradicts it,
- the claim exposes private or donor-level data,
- the claim overstates UC connection, wealth, capacity, philanthropy, or intent.

## Special Rules

### UC Connection

Approve only with explicit evidence. Do not infer from:

- Canterbury location,
- relevant sector,
- plausible research overlap,
- likely alumni status,
- shared interests,
- event attendance without a stated UC link,
- a leader's UC background as proof of an organisation's UC connection.

### Capacity and Wealth

Accept public indicators only as indicators. Do not convert them into wealth estimates, donor likelihood, giving capacity, or philanthropic intent.

### Sensitivities

Sensitive material needs stronger evidence and a clear reason to include. If it is not necessary for the briefing, mark it internal-only or exclude it.
